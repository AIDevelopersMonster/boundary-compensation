# Boundary Compensation Origin I: Oriented Winding Shadows, Structural Scale Selection, and Signed Spectral Localization

## Abstract

This paper introduces the first constructive toy model of the Boundary Compensation Origin Program (BC-Origin). The program studies whether observable elementary structures can be represented as non-fundamental shadows of hidden residual generators, with observable scale and sign information selected by structural compatibility equations rather than imposed as free cutoffs. The minimal hidden generator is modeled as an index-winding pair \(h=(q,n)\), where \(q\) is an index-like residual type and \(n\in\mathbb Z\) is an oriented winding number. The winding number induces two observable channels: a scale channel \(|n|\) and an orientation channel \(\operatorname{sgn}(n)\). A phase-closure equation selects a discrete observable scale ratio, while a signed two-shadow spectral operator converts the orientation product \(\operatorname{sgn}(n_1n_2)\) into localization or delocalization of observable branches. The resulting toy model produces three mathematically explicit effects: orientation-controlled shadow localization, an admissibility horizon, and a coupling-induced shadow gap. The model is not proposed as a final physical hidden sector; it is a controlled structural-existence model showing how observable scale, sign, and local admissibility can arise from hidden oriented residual data.

## 1. Introduction

The Boundary Compensation program began with the idea that observed structures may be residues of compensation between hidden boundary regimes. In its certification-oriented form, the program studies the stability of finite-resolution claims under thresholds, atlases, quotient choices, perturbations, and representational ambiguity. BC-Origin addresses a different question: can an observed elementary layer be modeled as a projected shadow of hidden residual generators?

The goal of this first paper is deliberately minimal. We do not attempt to reconstruct a true hidden sector, nor to identify a physical particle model. Instead, we construct a finite-dimensional spectral toy model in which an observable shadow is generated by hidden structural data. The central requirement is that observable scales and signs should be selected by equations and projections, not assigned as arbitrary cutoffs.

The model starts from an oriented winding number on a hidden circle. This winding number has two natural projections: its magnitude and its orientation. The magnitude controls a resonant closure denominator and therefore an observable scale; the orientation controls a sign channel that can enter a signed spectral operator. This gives the first BC-Origin mechanism:

```text
hidden oriented winding -> scale channel + sign channel -> observable shadow
```

The paper then studies the two-shadow case. A signed common-shift operator produces orientation-controlled displacement of inverse-scale denominators. When a denominator reaches zero, the associated observable scale diverges and the branch ceases to define a localized shadow. Off-diagonal coupling separates the two spectral branches and produces a reproducible gap.

## 2. Hidden generators and shadow channels

Let a hidden residual generator be represented by

```text
h = (q, n)
```

where `q` is an index-like residual type and `n in Z` is an oriented winding number. The winding number is not observed directly. Instead, it induces two observable channels:

```text
scale channel: |n|
sign channel:  sign(n)
```

The shadow map is written schematically as

```text
Pi(h) = (Q(h), N(h), Sigma(h), S(h))
```

with

```text
Q(h)     = q
N(h)     = |n|
Sigma(h) = sign(n)
S(h)     = ell_h / L
```

The additive structure belongs to the hidden winding number, not directly to its sign projection. Thus

```text
n_total = n1 + n2
sign_total = sign(n_total)
```

rather than `sign(n1)+sign(n2)`. Multiplicative interaction signs, however, are naturally represented by

```text
sign(n1*n2) = sign(n1)*sign(n2)
```

for nonzero windings.

## 3. One-generator closure model

Let the inverse-scale spectral function be

```text
lambda_q(ell) = mu_|q| / ell
```

where `mu_|q| > 0`. The closure equation is

```text
lambda_q(ell)*L + theta(|q|) = 2*pi*|n|
```

The admissibility condition is

```text
2*pi*|n| - theta(|q|) > 0
```

Solving for the observable scale ratio gives

```text
ell/L = mu_|q| / (2*pi*|n| - theta(|q|))
```

### Proposition 1: Oriented winding shadow decomposition

For nonzero `n`, the oriented winding number decomposes into two observable shadow channels: `|n|` and `sign(n)`.

### Proposition 2: Structural scale selection

For fixed `(q,n)` satisfying the admissibility condition, the observable scale ratio is uniquely selected by the closure equation.

### Proposition 3: Discrete shadow scale family

For fixed `q`, the admissible observable scale ratios form a discrete family indexed by `|n|`.

## 4. Two-shadow signed spectral operator

For two hidden generators

```text
h1 = (q1, n1)
h2 = (q2, n2)
```

define

```text
d_i = 2*pi*|n_i| - theta(|q_i|)
s   = sign(n1*n2)
```

The signed common-shift operator is

```text
D_signed = [[d1 + gamma*s, kappa],
            [kappa,        d2 + gamma*s]]
```

The eigen-denominators are

```text
lambda_pm = (d1+d2)/2 + gamma*s +/- sqrt(((d1-d2)/2)^2 + kappa^2)
```

Observable scale ratios are interpreted as

```text
S_pm = mu_eff / lambda_pm
```

on branches satisfying `lambda_pm > 0`.

## 5. Core effects

### 5.1 Orientation-controlled shadow localization

For `gamma > 0`, same-oriented winding products (`s=+1`) shift both inverse-scale denominators upward:

```text
s = +1 -> lambda_pm increases -> S_pm decreases
```

Opposite-oriented winding products (`s=-1`) shift them downward:

```text
s = -1 -> lambda_pm decreases -> S_pm increases
```

Thus the hidden orientation product controls localization or delocalization of observable shadows.

### 5.2 Admissibility horizon

A localized observable branch requires

```text
lambda_pm > 0
```

At

```text
lambda_pm = 0
```

the corresponding scale ratio diverges:

```text
S_pm -> infinity
```

This boundary is called the admissibility horizon. It marks loss of localized observability for that branch within the toy model.

For the lower opposite-orientation branch, writing

```text
lambda_base = (d1+d2)/2
R = sqrt(((d1-d2)/2)^2 + kappa^2)
```

the critical value is

```text
gamma_c = lambda_base - R
```

when `gamma_c > 0`.

### 5.3 Coupling-induced shadow gap

The branch separation is

```text
lambda_plus - lambda_minus = 2*sqrt(((d1-d2)/2)^2 + kappa^2)
```

The off-diagonal coupling `kappa` therefore produces a reproducible spectral gap. This gap separates branches; it does not automatically protect every branch from crossing the admissibility horizon.

## 7. Structural coupling problem

The main weakness of the present model is the external appearance of `gamma` and `kappa`. In BC-Origin I they are explicit operator parameters. To avoid fitted-number contamination, the next stage of the program must derive coupling coefficients from structural overlap operators.

A first candidate is to use hidden circle modes

```text
u_n(phi) = exp(i*n*phi)/sqrt(2*pi)
```

and a canonical circle operator

```text
A = (1 - Delta_S1)^(-1)
```

which yields

```text
kappa_ij = <u_ni, A u_nj> = 1/(1 + (n_i-n_j)^2)
```

This turns the coupling coefficient into a structural value generated by the winding data and a fixed kernel.

## 8. Multi-shadow handoff

The extension to `N` shadows should not be described as genuine spin-glass frustration if the only edge sign is

```text
s_ij = sign(n_i*n_j)
```

because this sign structure factorizes as

```text
s_ij = sigma_i*sigma_j
```

and every nonzero triangle has positive cycle product:

```text
s_ij*s_jk*s_ki = +1
```

The resulting sign graph is balanced. Genuine frustration would require a non-factorizable edge sign or nontrivial cycle holonomy. This is a later extension, not a theorem of BC-Origin I.

## 9. Claim status

The following claims are theorems inside the toy model:

1. oriented winding decomposes into magnitude and sign channels;
2. the one-generator closure equation selects a unique admissible scale;
3. the signed two-shadow operator produces orientation-controlled spectral displacement;
4. zero denominator defines an admissibility horizon;
5. off-diagonal coupling produces branch separation.

The following are structural interpretations:

1. observable shadows may be non-fundamental projections of hidden residual data;
2. localization and delocalization may be represented as inverse-scale spectral effects;
3. horizon crossing represents loss of localized observability.

The following are critic targets:

1. the physical origin of the closure equation;
2. the structural derivation of `gamma` and `kappa`;
3. the absence of time evolution;
4. the extension from balanced sign geometry to genuine non-factorizable holonomy.

## 10. Conclusion

BC-Origin I establishes the first practical building block of the Boundary Compensation Origin Program: oriented winding shadows, structural scale selection, signed spectral localization, an admissibility horizon, and a coupling-induced branch gap. The model is intentionally minimal, but it is computationally reproducible and mathematically explicit. The natural next step is BC-Origin II: deriving coupling coefficients from structural kernels and extending the analysis to multi-shadow spectral geometry.
