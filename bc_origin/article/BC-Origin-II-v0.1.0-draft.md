## Abstract

This paper replaces the free pairwise coupling parameter of the Boundary Compensation Origin I (BC-Origin I) toy model with structural coupling kernels defined on a hidden winding-index space. BC-Origin I introduced oriented winding shadows, structural scale selection, and a signed two-shadow spectral operator containing an explicit off-diagonal parameter \(\kappa\). Here we replace this pairwise constant by a kernel matrix \(K_{ij}=K(n_i,n_j)\), so that coupling strengths are determined by hidden winding-index data once a structural kernel has been fixed. This yields a multi-shadow signed spectral operator \(D_N\) whose spectra, admissibility horizons, and localization patterns are governed by kernel geometry rather than pairwise numerical fitting. The construction preserves the claim discipline of BC-Origin I: the results are theorem-level statements inside the toy model, while the choice of kernel remains a structural modelling assumption. The baseline sign graph \(s_{ij}=\operatorname{sgn}(n_i n_j)\) is shown to be balanced, so the model does not produce genuine spin-glass frustration without an additional non-factorizable edge holonomy.

## 1. Introduction

The BC-Origin program explores whether observable elementary structures can be modeled as non-fundamental projections, or shadows, of hidden residual generators. In BC-Origin I, a minimal hidden generator was represented as \(h=(q,n)\), where \(q\) is an index-like residual type and \(n\in\mathbb Z\setminus\{0\}\) is an oriented winding index. The shadow map decomposed \(n\) into a scale channel \(|n|\) and an orientation channel \(\operatorname{sgn}(n)\). A phase-closure equation selected discrete observable scales, and a signed \(2\times2\) spectral operator modeled orientation-controlled localization.

The remaining weakness of BC-Origin I is the explicit off-diagonal coupling parameter \(\kappa\). If \(\kappa\) is chosen pair by pair to obtain a desired spectrum, the model loses its structural character. The purpose of BC-Origin II is to replace \(\kappa\) by a structural kernel evaluated on the hidden winding-index space.

The central transition is

\[
\kappa \quad\longrightarrow\quad \kappa_{ij}=K(n_i,n_j).
\]

This does not remove every modelling choice: the kernel itself is a structural primitive or must be derived from a deeper hidden geometry. But it does remove pairwise fitted constants and makes multi-shadow spectra reproducible from hidden labels and a declared kernel rule.

## 2. Recap of BC-Origin I

A hidden generator is

\[
h=(q,n), \qquad n\in\mathbb Z\setminus\{0\}.
\]

The winding index decomposes as

\[
n\mapsto (|n|,\operatorname{sgn}(n)).
\]

The one-generator closure equation gives

\[
\frac{\ell}{L}=\frac{\mu_{|q|}}{2\pi|n|-\theta(|q|)}.
\]

For two shadows, with

\[
s=\operatorname{sgn}(n_1n_2), \qquad d_i=2\pi|n_i|-\theta(|q_i|),
\]

BC-Origin I used

\[
D_{\rm signed}=\begin{pmatrix}
d_1+\gamma s & \kappa\\
\kappa & d_2+\gamma s
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The model produced three effects: orientation-controlled shadow localization, an admissibility horizon, and a coupling-induced shadow gap.

## 3. The coupling problem

In the two-shadow model, the off-diagonal term \(\kappa\) controls the branch separation

\[
\lambda_+-\lambda_-=2\sqrt{\left(\frac{d_1-d_2}{2}\right)^2+\kappa^2}.
\]

If \(\kappa\) is merely selected numerically, the model becomes phenomenological. BC-Origin requires that observed quantities be selected by structural compatibility equations, kernels, overlaps, or selection rules. Therefore the coupling should be generated by hidden data and a declared structural kernel.

## 4. Hidden winding-index space

Let the hidden winding labels lie in

\[
\mathcal I=\mathbb Z\setminus\{0\}.
\]

For an \(N\)-shadow configuration, fix a finite subset

\[
\mathcal I_N=\{n_1,\dots,n_N\}\subset\mathcal I.
\]

A structural kernel is a symmetric function

\[
K:\mathcal I\times\mathcal I\to\mathbb R
\]

with finite restriction \(K_{ij}=K(n_i,n_j)\). In the baseline case we use translation-invariant kernels

\[
K_{ij}=k(n_i-n_j).
\]

The desired properties are

\[
K_{ij}=K_{ji},\qquad K_{ij}\ge0,\qquad K_{ii}=1,
\]

and

\[
K_{ij}\to0\quad\text{as}\quad |n_i-n_j|\to\infty.
\]

## 5. Structural kernel choices

Several admissible kernel constructions are possible.

### 5.1 Positive-definite Gram kernels

One may declare a positive-definite Toeplitz kernel on the label lattice:

\[
K_{ij}=k(n_i-n_j).
\]

Examples include exponentially decaying kernels and Lorentzian-type kernels:

\[
k(m)=e^{-\alpha |m|},\qquad k(m)=\frac{1}{1+m^2}.
\]

In this approach the kernel is a structural primitive of the model, not a pairwise fitted number.

### 5.2 Lattice-resolvent kernels

A more operator-oriented construction uses a discrete Laplacian on the winding-index lattice. For example, with an appropriate positive operator \(A_{\mathbb Z}\) on \(\ell^2(\mathbb Z)\), define

\[
K=(A_{\mathbb Z})^{-1}.
\]

Then \(K_{ij}\) is an operator kernel on the label space. This is distinct from the matrix element of \((1-\Delta_{S^1})^{-1}\) between pure Fourier modes on the circle.

### 5.3 Important correction: Fourier-mode diagonalization

If \(u_n(\phi)=e^{in\phi}/\sqrt{2\pi}\) are pure Fourier modes on \(S^1\), then

\[
(1-\Delta_{S^1})^{-1}u_{n_j}=\frac{1}{1+n_j^2}u_{n_j},
\]

and hence

\[
\langle u_{n_i},(1-\Delta_{S^1})^{-1}u_{n_j}\rangle=\frac{1}{1+n_j^2}\delta_{ij}.
\]

Thus an off-diagonal kernel such as \(K_{ij}=1/(1+(n_i-n_j)^2)\) is not obtained from pure Fourier matrix elements of this diagonal operator. It must instead be formulated as an index-space kernel, a smeared-packet overlap, or a positive-definite Gram kernel.

## 6. Multi-shadow signed spectral operator

For \(N\) shadows define

\[
s_{ij}=\operatorname{sgn}(n_i n_j),\qquad d_i=2\pi|n_i|-\theta(|q_i|).
\]

A baseline multi-shadow operator is

\[
(D_N)_{ii}=d_i+\gamma\sum_{j\ne i}s_{ij}K_{ij},
\]

\[
(D_N)_{ij}=\eta K_{ij},\qquad i\ne j.
\]

Here \(\gamma\) and \(\eta\) are global operator-scale normalizations, not pairwise fitted constants. They can be fixed by normalization conventions or varied as global model controls.

## 7. Theorems inside the toy model

### Theorem 1 — Structural coupling replacement

Given a fixed structural kernel \(K\), the pairwise couplings are

\[
\kappa_{ij}=K_{ij}.
\]

Thus pairwise couplings are determined by hidden winding labels and the declared kernel, not by pairwise numerical fitting.

### Theorem 2 — Self-adjointness

If \(K_{ij}=K_{ji}\) and all \(d_i,\gamma,\eta,s_{ij}\) are real, then \(D_N\) is real symmetric. Consequently all eigen-denominators \(\lambda_a(D_N)\) are real.

### Theorem 3 — BC-Origin I recovery

For \(N=2\), the multi-shadow operator reduces to a BC-Origin-I-type signed two-shadow operator with off-diagonal coupling

\[
\kappa=\eta K_{12}.
\]

The diagonal signed shift is \(\gamma s_{12}K_{12}\) in the baseline normalization. Exact agreement with the BC-Origin I convention is obtained either when \(K_{12}=1\) or by absorbing \(K_{12}\) into the effective two-shadow shift coefficient.

### Theorem 4 — Admissibility horizon

A branch is localized when its eigen-denominator is positive:

\[
\lambda_a(D_N)>0.
\]

The global admissibility horizon is

\[
\lambda_{\min}(D_N)=0.
\]

If \(\lambda_{\min}(D_N)<0\), at least one branch no longer defines a localized observable shadow in the scale-ratio interpretation.

### Theorem 5 — Kernel locality

If \(K_{ij}\to0\) as \(|n_i-n_j|\to\infty\), then distant winding labels weakly couple. For configurations separated into distant clusters, \(D_N\) approaches block-diagonal form up to small kernel tails.

## 9. Balanced signs and non-frustration

The baseline sign rule factorizes:

\[
s_{ij}=\operatorname{sgn}(n_i)\operatorname{sgn}(n_j)=\sigma_i\sigma_j.
\]

Therefore every nonzero triangle satisfies

\[
s_{ij}s_{jk}s_{ki}=+1.
\]

The sign graph is balanced. Although \(N\ge3\) can produce multi-branch localization patterns, the baseline model does not produce genuine spin-glass topological frustration. Genuine frustration requires a non-factorizable edge holonomy \(\varepsilon_{ij}\) with nontrivial cycle products.

## 10. Bridge to BC-Core

The operator \(D_N\) is a finite-dimensional self-adjoint matrix generated from hidden residual labels and structural kernels. This gives a natural bridge to BC-Core: perturbations of \(K\), \(d_i\), \(\gamma\), and \(\eta\) can define uncertainty tubes, while horizon crossing \(\lambda_{\min}=0\) acts as a threshold wall. A full certification treatment is a later BC-Bridge task, not the central goal of BC-Origin II.

## 11. Claim status

### Theorems inside the model

- self-adjointness of \(D_N\);
- structural replacement of pairwise \(\kappa\) by \(K_{ij}\);
- admissibility horizon condition;
- \(N=2\) recovery up to normalization convention;
- kernel locality and approximate block decoupling.

### Structural interpretations

- coupling emerges from hidden index-space geometry once a kernel is fixed;
- horizon maps define admissibility regions for multi-shadow spectra;
- structural kernels replace pairwise fitting by model-level geometry.

### Non-claims and critic targets

- the specific kernel choice remains a modelling assumption unless derived from deeper hidden geometry;
- \(\gamma\) and \(\eta\) remain global scale normalizations;
- the model has no real-time dynamics;
- genuine frustration requires non-factorizable holonomy;
- this is not a derivation of gravity, the Standard Model, or physical particle spectra.

## 12. Conclusion

BC-Origin II removes the main phenomenological weakness of BC-Origin I by replacing the free pairwise coupling \(\kappa\) with a structural kernel matrix \(K_{ij}\) on hidden winding-index space. The resulting \(N\)-shadow spectral operator determines admissibility horizons and localization patterns from declared kernel geometry and hidden winding data rather than from pairwise numerical fitting. This establishes the next constructive layer of the BC-Origin program and prepares the path toward structural multi-shadow geometry and later certification by BC-Core methods.
